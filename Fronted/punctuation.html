Punctuation Rules Guide

Introduction to Punctuation

Punctuation marks are symbols that help organize written language and make its meaning clear. They indicate pauses, stops, questions, exclamations, and relationships between parts of sentences.

Why Punctuation Matters

Proper punctuation:

  • Clarifies meaning
  • Indicates pauses and intonation
  • Shows relationships between ideas
  • Makes writing easier to read
  • Conveys the writer's tone

Example of punctuation changing meaning:

"Let's eat, Grandma!" vs "Let's eat Grandma!"

The comma makes the difference between a friendly suggestion and cannibalism!

End Punctuation Marks

Period (.)

Used to:

  • End declarative sentences The sun is shining.
  • End imperative sentences Close the door.
  • Indicate abbreviations Dr. Smith, U.S.A., etc.

Question Mark (?)

Used to:

  • End direct questions What time is it?
  • Show uncertainty He was 45(?) years old.

Do not use for indirect questions: She asked what time it was.

Exclamation Point (!)

Used to:

  • Express strong emotion Watch out!
  • Show emphasis That's amazing!
  • After interjections Wow! That's incredible.

Use sparingly in formal writing. Multiple exclamation points (!! or !!!) are generally inappropriate in professional contexts.

Commas and Semicolons

Comma (,)

Main uses:

  1. Separate items in a list We bought apples, oranges, and bananas.
  2. Set off introductory elements After the movie, we went home.
  3. Separate coordinate adjectives It was a cold, windy day.
  4. Set off nonessential clauses My brother, who lives in Paris, is visiting.
  5. Before coordinating conjunctions in compound sentences I wanted to go, but I was too tired.

Semicolon (;)

Main uses:

  1. Join closely related independent clauses The rain stopped; the sun came out.
  2. Separate items in a complex list We visited Paris, France; Rome, Italy; and Madrid, Spain.

A semicolon connects two complete sentences that could stand alone but are closely related.

Common comma mistake: The "Oxford comma" (comma before 'and' in a list) is preferred in formal writing.

With Oxford comma: I love my parents, Lady Gaga, and Humpty Dumpty.

Without Oxford comma: I love my parents, Lady Gaga and Humpty Dumpty. (This suggests your parents are Lady Gaga and Humpty Dumpty!)

Colons, Dashes, and Hyphens

Colon (:)

Used to:

  • Introduce a list Bring these items: a pen, paper, and your ID.
  • Introduce an explanation or example She had one goal: to win.
  • Separate hours from minutes 3:30 PM
  • After the salutation in business letters Dear Sir:

Dash (— or --)

Used to:

  • Indicate a sudden break or change in thought I was going to—wait, what was that noise?
  • Set off parenthetical information Three countries—France, Italy, and Spain—border the Mediterranean.
  • Emphasize what follows There's only one person who can help—you.

Hyphen (-)

Used to:

  • Join compound words mother-in-law, well-known
  • Connect prefixes in some cases ex-president, self-esteem
  • Write out numbers 21-99 twenty-one, forty-two

Apostrophes and Quotation Marks

Apostrophe (')

Used to:

  1. Show possession Sarah's book, the dogs' leashes
  2. Form contractions don't, can't, it's

Common mistakes:

  • Its (possessive) vs. it's (contraction for "it is")
  • Plurals don't need apostrophes (NOT: banana's for sale)

Quotation Marks (" " or ' ')

Used to:

  • Enclose direct speech She said, "Hello."
  • Indicate titles of short works "The Raven" (poem), "Yesterday" (song)
  • Show irony or special usage His "help" made things worse.

Single quotation marks are used for quotes within quotes: He said, "I heard her say 'no' clearly."

Punctuation with Quotation Marks

In American English:

  • Commas and periods always go inside quotation marks She said, "Come here."
  • Colons and semicolons go outside He called her "friend"; she disagreed.
  • Question marks and exclamation points go inside if part of the quote, outside if not Did she say "I'm coming"? vs. She asked, "Are you coming?"
